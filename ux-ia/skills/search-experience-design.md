# Search Experience Design
**Purpose:** Design search that works the way users think — not just returning results but understanding intent, handling ambiguity, and getting users to what they need fast.

Search is the escape hatch. When navigation fails, users search. When the product is complex, users search. When users know exactly what they want, they search. This makes search the most revealing feature in any product — search logs tell you exactly what users want, in their own words, and whether they found it.

## The Query Understanding
Users don't search the way databases work. They use natural language, make typos, use different words for the same thing, and often don't know exactly what they're looking for. Good search handles all of this — autocomplete that guides, spell-correction that forgives, synonyms that translate, and results that disambiguate.

## The Results Page Design
The results page is a design challenge, not a technical one. Relevance ranking, result display format, filtering and faceting, "no results" handling, and progressive disclosure of advanced options — each of these is a design decision that affects whether users find what they need.

## The No-Results Problem
"No results found" is the worst possible search experience. Every no-results page should offer alternatives: did-you-mean suggestions, popular searches, browse alternatives, or contact support. The goal is to never leave the user at a dead end.

## When to use
When the product has enough content that navigation alone is insufficient. When search logs reveal high failure rates. When users report difficulty finding content. When adding new content types to an existing product.
