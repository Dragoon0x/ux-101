# Design Principles
**Purpose:** Define principles that actually guide decisions — not generic platitudes but specific, opinionated statements that help the team choose between competing design options.

"Simple and intuitive" is not a design principle. Every product in the world claims to be simple and intuitive. A real design principle is specific enough to be controversial — meaning reasonable people could disagree with it. "We sacrifice feature completeness for first-use clarity" is a principle because it expresses a specific tradeoff. "We prioritize expert efficiency over beginner learnability" is a principle because it names who the design serves.

## The Tradeoff Test
A useful design principle names a tradeoff. It says "we choose A over B" where both A and B are valid options. "We choose speed over polish" guides decisions. "We value quality" guides nothing because no one would argue the opposite.

## The Decision Test
After writing a principle, test it against three recent design decisions. Does the principle help you choose between the options? If the principle supports both options equally, it's too vague. If it clearly favors one option, it's working.

## The Memorable Test
Principles that the team can't remember are principles that don't influence decisions. Keep them short — 8 words or fewer. Keep them few — 5-7 principles maximum. Keep them vivid — language that creates a mental image sticks better than abstract language.

## When to use
At the start of a product or major redesign. When the team keeps having the same design debates. When new designers join and need to understand the design philosophy. When the product has grown inconsistently and needs realignment.
