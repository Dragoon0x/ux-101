# Design Okrs
**Purpose:** Set design OKRs that measure experience quality, not design team output — because "shipped 12 features" tells you nothing about whether the product got better.

Design OKRs should measure the impact of design on user experience, not the volume of design production. The number of screens designed, components built, or features shipped are output metrics. The quality of the user experience is the outcome metric, and outcomes are what OKRs should measure.

## Good Design OKRs
Objective: "Make onboarding effortless." KR1: Reduce time-to-first-value from 12 minutes to 5 minutes. KR2: Increase Day-7 retention from 34% to 45%. KR3: Reduce support tickets tagged 'getting started' by 60%. These are measurable, time-bound, and directly connected to experience quality.

## Bad Design OKRs
"Redesign the dashboard" (it's a task, not an outcome). "Improve design quality" (unmeasurable). "Ship the design system" (output, not outcome). "Increase NPS" (too distant from design actions to be useful as a quarterly target).

## The Measurement Gap
The hardest part of design OKRs is measurement. Task completion rate, time-on-task, error rate, and SUS (System Usability Scale) scores are directly measurable. Retention and conversion are indirectly influenced by design. Choose KRs where design is the primary lever, not one of twenty factors.
