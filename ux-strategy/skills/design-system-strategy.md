# Design System Strategy
**Purpose:** Plan and build a design system that serves the product's needs — not a component library that becomes a maintenance burden, but a living system that accelerates design and development while maintaining quality.

A design system is an investment. It costs significant effort to build, requires ongoing maintenance, and only pays off if the team actually uses it. The strategy determines whether the investment produces returns — whether the system accelerates work and improves quality, or becomes shelfware that nobody trusts.

## The Adoption-First Approach
The biggest design system risk is not quality — it's adoption. A perfect system that nobody uses is worthless. The strategy should prioritize adoption over completeness. Ship a small system that covers the most common patterns well. Get teams using it. Expand based on what teams actually need, not what the system team thinks is complete.

## The Governance Model
Who decides what goes into the system? Who reviews contributions? Who resolves disputes? Who maintains quality? Without governance, the system either stagnates (because nobody has authority to add to it) or degrades (because everyone adds to it without review). The governance model should be lightweight enough to not slow down contributors and rigorous enough to maintain quality.

## The Documentation Standard
Components without documentation are components nobody uses correctly. Documentation should include: when to use this component, when not to use it, the interaction specifications, the accessibility requirements, and live examples. Documentation is not a nice-to-have — it's the mechanism that makes the system usable.

## When to use
When multiple teams or products need consistent UI. When design-to-development handoff is slow or error-prone. When the product has grown inconsistently and needs unification. When scaling the design team and needing shared foundations.
