# UX Metrics & Measurement
**Purpose:** Measure user experience in ways that actually reflect experience quality — not vanity metrics that make the dashboard look good but miss what users actually feel.

What gets measured gets managed. If UX isn't measured, it doesn't get resourced. But measuring UX badly is worse than not measuring it — it optimizes for the wrong things. A team that optimizes for clicks might create a confusing interface that requires many clicks to accomplish simple tasks. A team that optimizes for time-on-site might create an interface that's hard to leave rather than valuable to use.

## The HEART Framework
Google's HEART framework measures: Happiness (subjective satisfaction), Engagement (behavioral depth), Adoption (new user uptake), Retention (continued use over time), and Task success (ability to complete goals). Each dimension captures a different aspect of experience quality. No single metric tells the whole story.

## The Task-Level Metrics
At the task level, measure: completion rate (can users do it?), time on task (how efficiently?), error rate (how accurately?), and satisfaction (how did it feel?). These four metrics together give a complete picture of task-level usability.

## The Leading vs Lagging Distinction
Retention and NPS are lagging indicators — they tell you about past experience. Task completion rates, error rates, and usability test findings are leading indicators — they predict future experience quality. Design teams should focus on leading indicators because they can be influenced before the damage reaches the lagging metrics.

## When to use
When establishing UX success criteria for a product or feature. When justifying design investment to stakeholders. When measuring whether design changes actually improved the experience. When comparing UX quality across products or competitors.
