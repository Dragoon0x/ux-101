# Form Design
**Purpose:** Design forms that users can complete quickly, accurately, and without frustration — the most common and most universally disliked interaction pattern.

Forms are where UX earns or loses the most money. Every signup form, checkout form, and data entry form is a conversion funnel with measurable drop-off at each field. The research is extensive and the principles are clear — yet most forms still violate basic best practices because form design is treated as layout rather than interaction design.

## The Field Reduction Principle
Every field you remove increases completion rate. The math is simple: fewer fields, less work, higher completion. For every field, ask: is this data essential right now, or can it be collected later? Forms that collect what's necessary for this step — not what the database wants — convert better.

## The Inline Validation
Validate as the user types, not after they submit. Inline validation catches errors at the moment they're made, when the user's attention is on that field. Submit-then-validate forces the user to scan the entire form to find the error, and often makes them re-enter data that was already correct.

## The Label Placement
Labels above fields outperform labels to the left for completion speed. Labels inside fields (placeholder text) fail because they disappear when the user starts typing, forcing recall instead of recognition. Labels that float from inside to above the field combine the space efficiency of placeholder text with the persistent visibility of above-field labels.

## The Error Design
Error messages should be specific, visible, and actionable. "Invalid input" is useless. "Email must include @ and a domain (e.g., name@company.com)" is actionable. Position error messages immediately adjacent to the field they reference. Use color and icons for visibility, but never color alone (for accessibility).

## When to use
Every form in every product. When form completion rates are below expectations. When support tickets mention form difficulty. When a new form needs to be created.
