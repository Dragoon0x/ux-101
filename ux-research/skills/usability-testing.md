# Usability Testing
**Purpose:** Test whether real users can actually use the thing you designed — finding the problems before your entire user base finds them for you.

Usability testing is the closest thing to a reality check that design has. It reveals the gap between how the designer thinks the product works and how actual humans experience it. That gap is always larger than expected, and the problems it reveals are almost never the problems the team predicted.

## The Task Design
The task is the test. A poorly designed task produces misleading results. Good tasks describe the user's goal without describing the interface path. "Find and buy a gift for a friend's birthday under $50" is a good task. "Click the gift finder button, then use the price filter" is not — it tests whether users can follow instructions, not whether the interface is usable.

## The Think-Aloud Protocol
Ask participants to narrate their thinking as they work through tasks. "Tell me what you're looking at, what you're thinking, what you expect to happen." Think-aloud reveals the mental model — how the user understands the interface — which is far more valuable than whether they succeeded or failed. A user who succeeds but is confused has found a problem that will affect others.

## The Observer Discipline
Watch. Don't help. The hardest part of usability testing is watching someone struggle with your design without intervening. The moment you explain something, you've invalidated the test. If the participant is genuinely stuck, note the failure point, then help them past it so you can test the rest of the flow.

## The Severity Framework
Not all usability problems are equal. A confusing icon label is a minor issue. An invisible primary action is a critical issue. Rank findings by severity: critical (prevents task completion), major (causes significant delay or confusion), minor (causes brief hesitation). Fix criticals before launch, majors in the next sprint, minors when resources allow.

## When to use
Before launching new features or products. After significant redesigns. When support tickets indicate usability problems. Regularly as part of the design process — not just at the end.
