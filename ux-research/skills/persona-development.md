# Persona Development
**Purpose:** Build personas that actually influence design decisions — not demographic profiles that get printed and forgotten, but behavioral models that the team uses daily.

Most personas are useless. They describe age, location, job title, and a stock photo — information that doesn't help a designer make a single decision. Useful personas describe behaviors, goals, frustrations, and decision patterns that differ between user types in ways that require different design solutions.

## The Behavioral Foundation
Personas should be built from observed behavior, not demographics. Two 35-year-old marketing managers might have completely different behaviors with your product — one is a power user who wants efficiency, the other is an occasional user who wants simplicity. The behavior pattern matters. The age doesn't.

## The Design-Relevant Differences
A persona is only useful if the differences between personas lead to different design decisions. If Persona A and Persona B would be served by the same interface, they shouldn't be separate personas. The test: can you name a design decision that would be right for Persona A and wrong for Persona B? If not, merge them.

## The Living Document
Personas that are created once and never updated become fictional characters — disconnected from the evolving user base. Update personas when research reveals behavior changes, when the product serves new user segments, or when usage data contradicts persona assumptions.

## When to use
When the team needs shared understanding of who they're designing for. When design decisions are being made based on the team's own preferences rather than user needs. When the product serves multiple distinct user types that require different design approaches.
