# Diary Studies
**Purpose:** Understand how people use products over time and in real context — capturing the longitudinal, contextual experience that lab research and interviews miss.

Most research methods capture a moment — a usability test snapshot, an interview recollection. Diary studies capture behavior as it happens, in context, over days or weeks. This reveals patterns that point-in-time research can't: when people use the product, what triggers usage, how usage evolves, what causes abandonment, and how the product fits into the broader workflow.

## The Study Design
Define the duration (typically 1-4 weeks), the trigger for diary entries (time-based: "log every evening" or event-based: "log every time you use the product"), and the entry format (open-ended, structured, or photo-based). Shorter durations with more frequent entries capture detail. Longer durations with less frequent entries capture trends.

## The Entry Design
The entry should take less than 3 minutes to complete. Any longer and participants drop out or submit low-quality entries. Ask 3-5 specific questions that capture context (what were you doing?), behavior (what did you do in the product?), emotion (how did that feel?), and outcome (did you accomplish your goal?).

## The Engagement Problem
Diary study participation declines over time. Expect 20-30% dropout. Mitigate with: reminders (automated, not nagging), incentives (milestone-based, not just completion), and check-ins (brief calls that show participants their contributions matter). Over-recruit by 30% to ensure a viable final sample.

## When to use
When understanding the longitudinal experience matters — onboarding, habit formation, feature adoption. When context of use is important — mobile products, tools used in specific environments. When you need to understand triggers and routines around product usage.
