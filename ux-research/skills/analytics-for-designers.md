# Analytics for Designers
**Purpose:** Use quantitative data to inform design decisions — not to replace qualitative research but to complement it with scale, precision, and objectivity.

Analytics tell you what happened. Research tells you why. Neither is complete without the other. A designer who ignores analytics designs based on a sample of 5-15 interview participants and misses patterns that only emerge at scale. A designer who only uses analytics optimizes for metrics without understanding the human experience behind them.

## The Designer's Dashboard
Not every metric matters for design decisions. The designer's dashboard tracks: task completion rates (can users do the thing?), time on task (how efficient is the experience?), error rates (where do users make mistakes?), drop-off points (where do users abandon?), and user paths (how do users actually navigate vs. how the design expected them to?).

## The Funnel Analysis
Every significant user flow is a funnel. Map the steps from entry to completion and measure the conversion rate at each step. The step with the biggest drop-off is the biggest design opportunity. Don't guess where users struggle — the funnel shows you.

## The Segmentation Skill
Averages lie. "Average time on task: 45 seconds" might mean most users complete in 20 seconds and a frustrated minority takes 3 minutes. Segment by user type, experience level, device, and entry point. The segments reveal design opportunities that averages hide.

## The A/B Test Literacy
Designers don't need to run A/B tests, but they need to read them. Understanding statistical significance, sample size requirements, and the difference between a meaningful result and a random fluctuation prevents bad design decisions based on inconclusive data.

## When to use
When prioritizing which usability problems to fix first. When measuring whether a design change actually improved the experience. When exploring user behavior at scale. When quantitative evidence is needed to justify design investment.
