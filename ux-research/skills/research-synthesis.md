# Research Synthesis
**Purpose:** Turn raw research data into insights that actually change decisions — because research that sits in a report nobody reads is research that never happened.

Synthesis is where research becomes valuable. Raw interview transcripts, usability test recordings, survey responses — these are data, not insights. The synthesis process transforms scattered observations into patterns, patterns into insights, and insights into recommendations that the product team can act on.

## The Affinity Mapping Process
Write every notable observation on a separate note. Physically or digitally arrange the notes into clusters based on similarity. Name each cluster. The cluster names are your themes. The themes that contain the most notes and the most emotionally charged observations are your primary insights.

## The Insight Structure
An insight is not an observation. "Users couldn't find the search bar" is an observation. "Users expect search to be the primary navigation method, but the current design buries it below browse categories because the team assumed browse was the primary behavior" is an insight. The insight connects the observation to the mental model and implies a design direction.

## The So-What Test
Every insight must answer "so what?" — what does this mean for the product? If an insight doesn't imply an action, it's an interesting fact, not a useful insight. "Users feel overwhelmed by the dashboard" implies "simplify the dashboard." "Users check email first thing in the morning" implies nothing unless email is relevant to the product.

## The Stakeholder Translation
Research insights need to be translated for different audiences. Engineers need specifics: what's broken, where, and how to detect it. Product managers need priorities: which problems matter most and why. Executives need implications: what this means for the business and what investment it requires. One synthesis, three translations.

## When to use
After every research study. When making product decisions — synthesize existing research before commissioning new research. When multiple research studies have accumulated without being connected. When the team is debating user needs based on opinions rather than evidence.
