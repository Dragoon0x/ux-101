# User Interviewing
**Purpose:** Conduct interviews that reveal how people actually think, behave, and make decisions — not what they say they do, but what they actually do and why.

User interviews are the highest-resolution research method available. Nothing else gives you the depth of understanding that a well-conducted 45-minute conversation provides. But most user interviews produce surface-level insights because they ask surface-level questions. The skill is going deeper — past the rehearsed answers, past the post-hoc rationalizations, into the actual behaviors and motivations.

## The Warm-Up
The first five minutes determine the quality of the entire interview. The participant needs to feel safe, respected, and genuinely listened to. Start with context questions about them — their role, their typical day, their relationship with the problem space. This warm-up serves two purposes: it builds rapport, and it gives you context that makes later answers interpretable.

## The Critical Incident Technique
Don't ask people what they generally do. Ask them what they did last time. "Walk me through the last time you needed to [do the thing]." Specific incidents produce specific details. General questions produce general answers. The last-time question forces the participant to recall a real event rather than construct an idealized narrative.

## The Five Whys (Applied Gently)
When a participant describes a behavior, ask why. When they answer, ask why again. Not mechanically, not interrogatively — conversationally. "That's interesting — what made you choose that approach?" Each layer of why gets closer to the actual motivation, which is usually different from the initial explanation.

## The Silence Technique
After a participant answers, wait. Count to five in your head before asking the next question. The silence feels uncomfortable, and that discomfort often prompts the participant to elaborate — adding the detail, nuance, or honest admission that they wouldn't have offered if you'd immediately moved on.

## When to use
During discovery — understanding the problem space before designing solutions. When quantitative data shows what but not why. When designing for a user segment you don't personally represent. When existing assumptions about user behavior need validation.
