# Note Taking Methods
**Purpose:** Capture research observations accurately and efficiently — because insights lost in the moment are insights lost forever.

Note-taking in user research is a specific skill, not just typing fast. You need to capture what happened (behavior), what was said (quotes), and what you noticed (observations) — while keeping them clearly separated because they serve different purposes in synthesis.

## The Method
Use a three-column format: Timestamp | Observation (what happened) | Interpretation (what it might mean). Keep observations factual ("user clicked the back button three times") and interpretations separate ("user appears lost in the navigation"). This separation prevents confirmation bias from contaminating your raw data.

## The Verbatim Rule
Capture direct quotes for any statement that reveals motivation, frustration, or mental model. "I just expected it to be under Settings" is more valuable than your paraphrase "user couldn't find the feature." Quotes carry emotional weight that paraphrases lose, and they're more persuasive in stakeholder presentations.

## The Practical Setup
Record the session (with consent) so you can review later. Take notes in real-time anyway — the act of noting forces attention, and the notes become the index for finding moments in the recording. Use shorthand: [Q] for quotes, [B] for behavior, [!] for surprises, [?] for things to follow up on.
