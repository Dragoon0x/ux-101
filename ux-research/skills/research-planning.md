# Research Planning
**Purpose:** Plan research that answers the right questions with the right methods at the right time — because bad research is worse than no research; it gives you false confidence.

The most common research failure is not bad execution — it's answering the wrong question. A beautifully executed usability test on a feature nobody wants is a waste of everyone's time. Research planning starts with the decision the research needs to inform, works backward to the question that decision requires, and only then selects the method.

## The Decision-First Framework
Before any research, name the decision. "We need to decide whether to build Feature X or Feature Y for the next quarter." The decision determines the question: "Which feature addresses a more urgent user need?" The question determines the method: interviews to understand need urgency, not a survey to count preferences.

## Method Selection
Methods are tools, not rituals. Interviews reveal why. Surveys reveal how many. Usability tests reveal whether. Analytics reveal what. Diary studies reveal when and where. Each method has a resolution — the level of detail it can detect — and a cost. Match the method's resolution to the question's requirements and the cost to the decision's stakes.

## The Sample Size Question
Five users find 85% of usability problems. That's true and also misleading. Five users are enough for qualitative pattern detection — identifying themes, uncovering problems, generating hypotheses. Five users are not enough for statistical confidence — measuring satisfaction scores, comparing conversion rates, validating that a problem affects most users. The sample size depends on whether you need patterns or percentages.

## Research Timing
Research that arrives after the decision has been made is a report, not an input. Build research into the product development cadence so findings arrive when they can influence decisions. This means starting research before the design phase, not during it.

## When to use
Before every significant product decision. When the team disagrees about what users need. When usage data shows unexpected behavior. When entering a new market or user segment.
